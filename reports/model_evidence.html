4 · Validation method — why it proves real-world skill

Walk-forward backtest: the honest way to test a forecaster.
  • Train on everything up to month M, predict month M+1, score, then roll forward.
  • The model NEVER sees the test period during training — no leakage.
  • Repeated over 3 separate months → tests stability, not luck.
  • This mirrors exactly how it will run in production each night.
  • A single random train/test split would overstate accuracy for time series — we avoid it.

5 · Which metrics apply — and which DON'T

AUC / ROC / precision / recall are classification metrics. They do NOT apply to this regression task.

NOT used (classification only)

AUC, ROC curve, precision, recall, F1, confusion matrix, log-loss. These need yes/no labels. We predict a quantity, not a class. Reporting them here would be wrong.

USED (regression + quantile)

WMAPE, MAE, RMSE, Bias, R² — for the point forecast. Pinball loss + Coverage — for the P85/P95 safety levels. Skill vs baseline — proves it beats simple methods.

6 · WMAPE — headline accuracy

On average our forecast is off by 34% of volume — 16% better than today's method.
WMAPE = Σ|actual − forecast| / Σ|actual|
0.341
  • Total miss in units ÷ total real demand. Volume-weighted (big sellers count more).
  • Stable where plain MAPE blows up (near-zero demand days).
Good looks like: Retail demand: <0.40 is workable, <0.35 good. We are at 0.341.

7 · MAE — average miss in units

Typical daily miss is about 2.3 units per product-shop line.
MAE = mean( |actual − forecast| )
2.29 units
  • Plain-English error size. Same units as demand.
  • Robust to outliers (unlike RMSE).
Good looks like: Lower is better; judge against the item's typical daily volume.

8 · RMSE — penalises big misses

Large errors are rare; RMSE close to MAE means few wild misses.
RMSE = sqrt( mean( (actual − forecast)² ) )
5.09 units
  • Squares errors → punishes large mistakes harder than MAE.
  • RMSE 5.09 vs MAE 2.29: gap is moderate → error spread is controlled.
Good looks like: Want RMSE not much larger than MAE → no catastrophic outliers.

9 · Bias — over or under forecasting?

Almost unbiased — a tiny tendency to under-forecast (0.41 units).
Bias = mean( forecast − actual )
-0.41 units
  • Near 0 = balanced. Negative = slight under-forecast.
  • Matters for ordering: persistent bias would systematically over/under-stock.
Good looks like: Want close to 0. −0.41 on ~6-unit orders is negligible & correctable.

10 · R² — variance explained

The model explains 81% of the variation in demand.
R² = 1 − SS(residual) / SS(total)
0.811
  • 1.0 = perfect, 0 = no better than predicting the average.
  • 0.811 means most of the day-to-day demand swing is captured.
Good looks like: >0.7 strong for noisy daily retail demand. We are at 0.81.
Scatter: each dot = one outlet-product-day; tight along the green diagonal = accurate.

11 · Pinball loss — quantile quality

Our P50/P85/P95 levels are scored honestly with the metric built for quantiles.
Pinball(q) = mean( max( q·e , (q−1)·e ) ), e = actual − forecast
P50 1.145 · P85 0.827 · P95 0.446
  • The correct loss for quantile forecasts (asymmetric penalty).
  • Lower = sharper AND correctly-placed intervals.
Good looks like: Used to compare quantile models; lower is better.

12 · Coverage / Calibration — are the safety levels honest?

When we say '85% safe', demand stays under it 85.4% of the time. Calibrated.
Coverage(q) = share of actuals ≤ forecast at quantile q
P85 → 85.4% · P95 → 94.5% (P50 → 56.7%)
  • P85/P95 land on target → intervals are trustworthy.
  • P50 at 56.7% reflects the small under-bias (we round orders up safely).
Good looks like: Coverage ≈ stated quantile = well-calibrated → safe for stock buffers.

24 · What would prove us WRONG (falsifiability)

A credible model states how it could fail — and we monitor for it.
  • Accuracy on new months drifts above ~0.40 WMAPE → retrain trigger fires.
  • Coverage drifts off target → intervals no longer trustworthy → recalibrate.
  • Bias grows persistently → systematic mis-stock → investigate features.
  • Drift monitor (PSI) flags input shift the model hasn't seen → relearn.
  • All four are watched automatically; none are silent.
